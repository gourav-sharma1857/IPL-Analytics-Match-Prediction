What the Data Shows

✅ Scores rose sharply — but not everywhere equally

Average first-innings scores jumped from 162 to 189 runs after the rule (+26 runs). The increase is consistent across all 9 home grounds with sufficient data, from +21 at Delhi to +37 at Hyderabad — suggesting the rule itself, not just pitch conditions, is the primary driver. Chennai is the notable outlier at only +10, likely due to Chepauk's spin-friendly surface resisting the broader trend.

✅ Openers changed the most — not death-over finishers

The conventional wisdom is that Impact Player helps teams by bringing in a specialist death-over hitter. But the data says otherwise: opener strike rates jumped from 125 to 150 (+24 points), while death-over runs stayed essentially flat. Powerplay and middle-over scoring drove the entire increase — consistent with batters playing more freely from ball one, knowing a specialist replacement is available if they fall early.

✅ Boundaries up, dot balls down — wickets barely changed

Boundary % rose from 16% to 20% while dot ball % fell from 35% to 31%. Crucially, wickets per innings barely moved (6.09 → 6.27). Batters are scoring more freely without being dismissed meaningfully more often — which argues strongly for a tactical/psychological shift in batting approach, not just luck or pitch conditions.

✅ 200+ scores went from rare to routine

Before the rule, only 10% of first innings crossed 200. After, it's 41% — almost every other match. This is perhaps the most striking single number in the dataset.

⚠️ Correlation, not causation

Scoring was already trending upward before 2023, driven by better bats, evolving T20 batting techniques, and shorter boundaries at some venues. This analysis shows a clear acceleration after the rule was introduced, and the pattern (openers specifically, powerplay/middle overs specifically) is consistent with the rule's mechanism — but definitively proving causation would require a controlled experiment that cricket doesn't offer.
